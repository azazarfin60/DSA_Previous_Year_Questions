# 5.12 Construct Binary Search Tree (BST) from Postorder | Data structures

📺 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yC83nJ2OAI8)

---

## 📌 Executive Summary
This lecture explains how to construct a unique Binary Search Tree (BST) when **only** the Postorder traversal is given. The method is identical to Preorder construction, but we traverse the Postorder array from **right to left** because the root is always at the end.

---

## 🔑 The Core Principle
1. **Derive Inorder:** The Inorder traversal of a BST always yields the data in **ascending (sorted) order**. Sort the given Postorder array to get the Inorder sequence.
2. **Postorder Rule:** In Postorder (Left-Right-Root), the **root is always the rightmost element**.
3. **Scan Right-to-Left:** To find the root of any sub-problem, scan the Postorder sequence from right to left and pick the first element you encounter.

---

## 📐 Step-by-Step Construction

**Given Postorder:** `5, 17, 19, 18, 16, 70, 85, 60, 20`

### Step 1: Find the Inorder Traversal
Sort the Postorder sequence in ascending order:
**Inorder:** `5, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 60, 70, 85`

### Step 2: Build the Tree
*Rule: Scan Postorder from right to left. The first element encountered is the root. Inorder tells us what goes to the left and right subtrees.*

**1. Root of the whole tree:**
- Rightmost element of Postorder is **20**.
- Split Inorder at 20: 
  - Left subtree: `{5, 16, 17, 18, 19}`
  - Right subtree: `{60, 70, 85}`

**2. Left Subtree `{5, 16, 17, 18, 19}`:**
- Check Postorder from right-to-left: which of these comes *first*? It's **16**. So, 16 is the root of this subtree.
- Split Inorder at 16:
  - Left of 16: `{5}` → Single element, so 5 is the left child.
  - Right of 16: `{17, 18, 19}`

**3. Right of 16 `{17, 18, 19}`:**
- Check Postorder from right-to-left: which comes first? It's **18**. So, 18 is the root here.
- Split Inorder at 18:
  - Left of 18: `{17}` → 17 is left child.
  - Right of 18: `{19}` → 19 is right child.

*Current Left Subtree structure:*
```text
      20
     /  
   16
  /  \
 5   18
    /  \
  17    19
```

**4. Right Subtree of Root `{60, 70, 85}`:**
- Check Postorder from right-to-left: which comes first? It's **60**. So, 60 is the root here.
- Split Inorder at 60:
  - Left of 60: *none*
  - Right of 60: `{70, 85}`

**5. Right of 60 `{70, 85}`:**
- Check Postorder from right-to-left: which comes first? It's **85**. So, 85 is the root here.
- Split Inorder at 85:
  - Left of 85: `{70}` → 70 is left child.
  - Right of 85: *none*

### Final Resulting BST:
```text
          20
        /    \
      16      60
     /  \       \
    5   18      85
       /  \    /
     17   19  70
```

---

## 🚀 Alternative Method (Without Inorder)
Since it's a BST, you can actually construct it without writing down the Inorder sequence:
1. Read Postorder right-to-left.
2. First element (`20`) is root.
3. Keep scanning right-to-left: elements greater than 20 (`60, 85, 70`) go to the right subtree.
4. The first element you hit that is *less* than 20 (`16`) marks the start of the left subtree elements. 
5. Recursively apply this logic.

## 💡 Key Takeaways
- For Preorder: scan left-to-right.
- For Postorder: scan **right-to-left**.
- The tree structure matches exactly with the Preorder example (Video 5.11) because they are derived from the same underlying tree.
