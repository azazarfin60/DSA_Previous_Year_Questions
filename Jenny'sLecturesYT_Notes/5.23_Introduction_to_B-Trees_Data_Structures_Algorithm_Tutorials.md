## 📌 Executive Summary
A **B-Tree** (also known as a Balanced M-way Tree) is a generalization of a Binary Search Tree (BST). While a BST node can have at most 2 children and 1 key, a B-Tree node can have multiple keys and multiple children. 

The most defining characteristic of a B-Tree is that **it is strictly balanced**: all insertions happen at the leaf nodes, and the tree grows *upward*, ensuring that **all leaf nodes are always at the exact same level**.

---

## 📐 Properties of a B-Tree (Order $M$)

The "Order" ($M$) dictates the maximum capacity of the tree's nodes.

### 1. Children Limitations
- **Maximum Children:** Every node can have at most **$M$** children.
- **Minimum Children (Internal Nodes):** $\lceil M/2 \rceil$ *(Ceiling of $M/2$)*
- **Minimum Children (Root):** $2$ *(Unless the tree only has a root)*
- **Minimum Children (Leaf):** $0$

### 2. Key Limitations
*Rule of thumb: Keys are always one less than children.*
- **Maximum Keys:** Every node can have at most **$M - 1$** keys.
- **Minimum Keys (Internal Nodes):** $\lceil M/2 \rceil - 1$
- **Minimum Keys (Root):** $1$

### 3. Structural Rules
- All keys within a single node must be stored in **sorted (ascending) order**.
- All leaf nodes must exist at the same level.
- Like a BST, elements to the left of a key $K$ are smaller than $K$, and elements to the right are greater than $K$.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Exam Advantage:**
> Always write down these properties immediately when starting a B-Tree construction question. Calculate the specific Max Keys and Min Keys for the given order to avoid silly overflow mistakes during construction.
