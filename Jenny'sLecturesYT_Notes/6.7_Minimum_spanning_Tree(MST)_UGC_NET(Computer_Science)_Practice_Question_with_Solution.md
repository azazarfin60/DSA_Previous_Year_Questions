## 📌 Executive Summary
This document covers a popular conceptual problem often asked in competitive exams like **UGC NET** and **GATE** regarding Minimum Spanning Trees (MSTs) and edge weight transformations.

---

## ❓ The Problem Statement

**Given:**
- Let **$X$** be the total cost of the Minimum Spanning Tree for a graph $G(V, E)$.
- Let **$Y$** be the total cost of the Minimum Spanning Tree for a modified graph $G(V, E^2)$. 
- In $G(V, E^2)$, the vertices remain the same, but the weight of every edge is **squared** ($W_{new} = W^2$).

**Question:** What is the definite mathematical relationship between $X$ and $Y$?
1. $X \ge Y$
2. $X \le Y$
3. $X = Y$
4. None of these

---

## 💡 Step-by-Step Analysis & Solution

To solve this, we must test the hypothesis using boundary cases. Edge weights in a graph belong to the set of Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$), meaning they can be integers $>1$, exactly $1$, or fractions between $0$ and $1$.

### Case 1: Integer Weights ($W > 1$)
Assume an MST with 4 vertices, requiring exactly 3 edges.
- **Original Edge Weights ($E$):** `5, 3, 2`
- **Cost $X$ (Sum):** $5 + 3 + 2 = \mathbf{10}$
- **Squared Edge Weights ($E^2$):** $5^2=25,\ 3^2=9,\ 2^2=4$
- **Cost $Y$ (Sum):** $25 + 9 + 4 = \mathbf{38}$
- **Result for Case 1:** $Y > X$

*If you stop here, you might be tempted to choose Option 2 ($X \le Y$). But you must check fractions!*

### Case 2: Fractional Weights ($0 < W < 1$)
Assume the edge weights are decimals less than 1. When you square a fraction, the resulting value becomes *smaller*.
- **Original Edge Weights ($E$):** `0.5, 0.3, 0.2`
- **Cost $X$ (Sum):** $0.5 + 0.3 + 0.2 = \mathbf{1.0}$
- **Squared Edge Weights ($E^2$):** $0.5^2=0.25,\ 0.3^2=0.09,\ 0.2^2=0.04$
- **Cost $Y$ (Sum):** $0.25 + 0.09 + 0.04 = \mathbf{0.38}$
- **Result for Case 2:** $X > Y$

---

## 🎯 Conclusion & Final Answer

Since the relationship completely flips depending on whether the edge weights are greater than 1 or less than 1, we cannot define a universal relationship between $X$ and $Y$.

**Correct Answer:** Option 4 - **None of these**.

> **Exam Tip:** In competitive exams, whenever a transformation is applied to edge weights (like squaring, square root, multiplying by a negative number), always explicitly test **fractional numbers ($0.1$ to $0.9$)** and **negative numbers** as your counter-examples. Examiners design these questions specifically to trap students who only think of whole positive integers.
