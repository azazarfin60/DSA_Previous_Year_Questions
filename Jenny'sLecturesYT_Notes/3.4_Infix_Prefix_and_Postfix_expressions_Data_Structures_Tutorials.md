## 📌 Executive Summary
Expressions in computer science consist of **operands** (variables or constants) and **operators** (+, -, *, /, ^). How these are arranged dictates how a computer parses and evaluates them. There are three standard notations: **Infix**, **Prefix**, and **Postfix**. Converting between these notations and evaluating them are primary applications of the **Stack** data structure.

---

## 1️⃣ Infix Notation
**Format:** `Operand Operator Operand`
- **Example:** `A + B` or `5 + 1 * 6`
- **Characteristics:** This is the standard mathematical format humans use.
- **Drawback:** It is ambiguous without strict rules. To evaluate it correctly, a computer (and a human) must rely on **Precedence** and **Associativity** rules, or use parentheses. This makes it computationally expensive and difficult for a compiler to parse directly.

### Precedence & Associativity Rules
When evaluating an Infix expression, operators with higher precedence are evaluated first. If two operators have the same precedence, associativity breaks the tie.

| Priority | Operator | Symbol | Associativity |
| :---: | :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **1 (Highest)** | Parentheses | `( )` | N/A |
| **2** | Exponentiation (Power) | `^` | **Right to Left** $\leftarrow$ |
| **3** | Multiplication / Division | `*` , `/` | **Left to Right** $\rightarrow$ |
| **4 (Lowest)** | Addition / Subtraction | `+` , `-` | **Left to Right** $\rightarrow$ |

**Evaluation Example (Left to Right Associativity):**
`1 + 10 + 30 / 5`
1. Division `30 / 5` has highest precedence $\rightarrow$ `1 + 10 + 6`
2. Two `+` operators. Left-to-Right associativity means evaluate the left one first $\rightarrow$ `11 + 6`
3. Final Answer $\rightarrow$ `17`

**Evaluation Example (Right to Left Associativity):**
`2 ^ 2 ^ 3`
1. Right-to-Left associativity means evaluate the rightmost power first: `2 ^ 3 = 8`.
2. Next evaluate the remaining power: `2 ^ 8`.
3. Final Answer $\rightarrow$ `256`. *(Evaluating left-to-right would incorrectly yield 64)*.

## 2️⃣ Prefix Notation (Polish Notation)
**Format:** `Operator Operand Operand`
- **Example:** `+ A B`
- **Characteristics:** The operator is placed *before* its operands. 
- **Example Conversion:** 
  - Infix: `A * B + C`
  - Step 1 (Multiplication): `(* A B) + C`
  - Step 2 (Addition): `+ * A B C`
- **Advantage:** Unlike Infix, Prefix expressions do **not** require parentheses, precedence rules, or associativity rules to be evaluated unambiguously.

---

## 3️⃣ Postfix Notation (Reverse Polish Notation / RPN)
**Format:** `Operand Operand Operator`
- **Example:** `A B +`
- **Characteristics:** The operator is placed *after* its operands.
- **Example Conversion:**
  - Infix: `A * B + C`
  - Step 1 (Multiplication): `(A B *) + C`
  - Step 2 (Addition): `A B * C +`
- **Advantage:** Extremely fast and memory-efficient for computers to evaluate using a **Stack**. Like Prefix, it requires zero parentheses or precedence rules.

---

## 🆚 Summary Comparison

| Feature | Infix | Prefix (Polish) | Postfix (Reverse Polish) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Format** | `A + B` | `+ A B` | `A B +` |
| **Human Readability** | Easy | Difficult | Difficult |
| **Machine Readability** | Difficult | Easy | **Easiest (via Stack)** |
| **Needs Parentheses?** | Yes | No | No |
| **Needs Precedence?** | Yes | No | No |

> **Exam Tip:** In exams, you are frequently asked to define these three notations. Keep definitions to a single line showing the structural format, followed by a simple example (`A+B`, `+AB`, `AB+`), and explicitly state that Prefix and Postfix eliminate the need for BODMAS/Precedence rules, making them highly efficient for compiler design.
