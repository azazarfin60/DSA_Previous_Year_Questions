# 5.31 Insertion in B+ Tree of Order 5 | Data Structures

📺 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=115K8LwP-xY) *(Note: Placeholder link)*

---

## 📌 Executive Summary
Creating a B+ Tree of an odd order (like Order 5) eliminates the ambiguity of choosing a left or right bias during a split, as there is always a definitive median element. However, the fundamental B+ Tree property remains paramount: **all data must exist in the leaf nodes**, and internal nodes hold strictly non-duplicated index pointers.

---

## ⚙️ Construction Parameters
- **Order ($M$):** 5
- **Max Children:** 5
- **Max Keys per node:** $M - 1 = 4$
- **Median Identification:** The 3rd element in a sorted list of 5 elements is the definitive median.

---

## 📝 Exam Walkthrough: B+ Tree Order 5 Insertion

> **Task:** Construct a B+ Tree of Order 5 inserting the keys: `7, 10, 1, 23, 5, 15, 17, 9, 11...`

**1. Initial Insertions (Fill the root):**
Insert `7, 10, 1, 23` and sort them.
```text
[ 1 | 7 | 10 | 23 ]
```

**2. Insert 5 (Leaf Overflow):**
The node becomes `[ 1 | 5 | 7 | 10 | 23 ]`. This exceeds the maximum of 4 keys.
- **Median element:** `7`.
- **Action:** Because this is a *leaf node*, push `7` up to the parent index, but retain a copy of `7` in the right leaf.
```text
         [ 7 ]
        /     \
   [1|5]  ->  [7|10|23]
```

**3. Insert 15, 17 (Leaf Overflow):**
`15` goes to the right leaf. `17` goes to the right leaf.
The right leaf becomes `[ 7 | 10 | 15 | 17 | 23 ]`.
- **Median element:** `15`.
- **Action:** Push index `15` up to the parent. Retain `15` in the right leaf.
```text
         [ 7 | 15 ]
        /    |     \
   [1|5] -> [7|10] -> [15|17|23]
```

**4. Insert 9, 11 (Leaf Overflow):**
Insert `9` into the middle leaf. Insert `11` into the middle leaf.
The middle leaf becomes `[ 7 | 9 | 10 | 11 | 15 ]` *(Assume 15 was inserted for this example to cause overflow)*.
- **Median element:** `10`.
- **Action:** Push index `10` up to the parent. Retain `10` in the right leaf.
```text
         [ 7 | 10 | 15 ]
        /    |    |     \
   [1|5] -> [7|9] -> [10|11] -> [15|17|23]
```

---

## ⚠️ The Crucial Distinction: Internal Node Splitting

As you continue inserting elements, eventually, the internal node `[7 | 10 | 15 | ... ]` will reach 5 elements and overflow.

When an **Internal Node** overflows, it behaves like a standard B-Tree, NOT a B+ Tree leaf.
- **Why?** Internal nodes are just signposts. They don't hold real data.
- **Rule:** Find the median index, push it up to the new root, and **DO NOT** leave a copy in the child internal node.

> [!WARNING]
> **Exam Trap:**
> The most common mistake students make is duplicating the median key when splitting an *internal* node in a B+ Tree. **Duplication only occurs when a leaf node splits.** If you duplicate an internal node key, your entire tree structure will be marked wrong.
